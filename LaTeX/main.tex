\title{Exact Coincidence Probabilities for Two-Mode Squeezed Vacuum States with Bucket Detectors}% Force line breaks with \\

\author{Nico Sieber}

\date{\today}
\begin{abstract}
We present a generating-function derivation of the exact coincidence probability for a two-mode squeezed vacuum (TMSV) state produced by type-II spontaneous parametric down-conversion, transformed by a half-wave plate, and measured with bucket detectors. In contrast to treatments based on truncating the TMSV to the lowest photon-number terms, our approach accounts for the full state and includes linear loss through detector efficiencies. This yields closed-form expressions for the vacuum and coincidence probabilities in the presence of symmetric or asymmetric loss. The formalism provides an exact analytical description of coincidence measurements in polarization-based SPDC experiments with non-photon-number-resolving detectors.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Photon-pair sources based on type-II spontaneous parametric down-conversion (SPDC) are a standard platform for studying nonclassical light and polarization interference. In the degenerate regime, and under appropriate phase-matching and mode-overlap conditions, the emitted state is well described as a two-mode squeezed vacuum (TMSV) in the horizontal and vertical polarization modes. When this state is sent through polarization optics and measured with non-photon-number-resolving detectors, the experimentally relevant quantities are typically coincidence probabilities rather than resolved photon-number statistics.

In many practical analyses, coincidence probabilities for such setups are estimated by truncating the TMSV expansion to the lowest few photon-number terms. While this can be sufficient in the weak-pumping regime, it does not provide an exact description of the full state. This limitation becomes especially relevant when higher-order pair contributions are non-negligible or when one wishes to retain a fully analytical treatment in the presence of loss.

In this work, we derive an exact analytical expression for the coincidence probability of a type-II SPDC-generated TMSV state after a half-wave plate (HWP) and subsequent polarization-resolved detection with bucket detectors. The experimental configuration considered here consists of a pumped type-II ppKTP crystal producing degenerate photons, followed by a compensation crystal to correct temporal walkoff between the horizontal and vertical components. The generated state is then rotated by a HWP at angle 
$\vartheta$, spatially separated at a polarizing beam splitter (PBS), and finally measured by bucket detectors.

Our approach combines the POVM description of lossy bucket detectors with a generating-function treatment of the transformed TMSV state. In this way, all photon-number contributions are included exactly, and detector loss can be incorporated through mode-dependent efficiencies. The resulting formalism yields compact expressions for the vacuum probabilities entering the coincidence signal and therefore for the coincidence probability itself.


The paper is organized as follows. In Sec.~II, we review a simplified $\ket{1_H,1_V}$ example that illustrates the polarization transformation induced by the HWP and the interference mechanism at $\vartheta = \pi/8$. Section~III introduces the bucket-detector POVM and the treatment of loss. In Sec.~IV, we derive the transformed TMSV state in exponential form and evaluate the relevant probabilities analytically. The appendices collect standard identities and technical steps used in the derivation.



\section{\label{sec:s1} Simplified example}

Before we work on the full discussion involving the TMSV, we first show a simplified example of the calculation by only passing the state 
\begin{align}
\label{initial_state_simple}
    \ket{1_{H},1_{V}}=a_H^\dagger a_V^\dagger\ket{0,0}
\end{align}
through the HWP as depicted in figure \ref{fig:setup}. 
\begin{figure}[b]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\columnwidth]{ExperimentalSetupN00N.png}
    \caption{Schematic of the experimental setup discussed in this work.}
    \label{fig:setup}
\end{figure}
In equation (\ref{initial_state_simple}), $a_{H,V}^\dagger$ are the bosonic creation operators (for $H$- and $V$-polarized light) following the relation

\begin{align}
\label{Fock}
|n_H, n_V\rangle = \frac{(a_H^\dagger)^{n_H}(a_V^\dagger)^{n_V}}{\sqrt{n_H! \, n_V!}} \, |0,0\rangle
\end{align}

Assuming that the fast axis of the HWP is aligned with the horizontal axis, the Jones matrix is given by\cite{collett2005fieldguidepolarization}

\begin{align}
\label{Jones}
    U_{\text{HWP}}(\vartheta) = 
    \begin{pmatrix}
    \cos(2\vartheta) & \sin(2\vartheta) \\
    \sin(2\vartheta) & -\cos(2\vartheta) 
    \end{pmatrix},
\end{align}

with $\vartheta$ being the angle of the HWP. With (\ref{initial_state_simple}) and (\ref{Jones}) we get the transformation

\begin{align}
\label{Tansformation:trafo}
    \begin{pmatrix}
    a_{H,\text{new}}^\dagger  \\
    a_{V,\text{new}}^\dagger
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    \begin{pmatrix}
    \cos(2\vartheta) & \sin(2\vartheta) \\
    \sin(2\vartheta) & -\cos(2\vartheta) 
    \end{pmatrix}
    \cdot
    \begin{pmatrix}
    a_{H,\text{old}}^\dagger  \\
    a_{V,\text{old}}^\dagger
    \end{pmatrix},
\end{align}
which can be used to write the state after the HWP as
\begin{align}
\label{Transformation:state_simple}
    \ket{1_H,1_V}&=a_H^\dagger a_V^\dagger\ket{0,0}\notag\\
    &\overset{(\ref{Tansformation:trafo})}{\Rightarrow} \left(a_H^\dagger\cos(2\vartheta)+a_V^\dagger\sin(2\vartheta)\right)\notag\\
    &\times\left(a_H^\dagger\sin(2\vartheta)-a_V^\dagger\cos(2\vartheta)\right)\ket{0,0}.
\end{align}
Evaluating the terms of (\ref{Transformation:state_simple}) results in:
\begin{align}
    \cos(2\vartheta)\sin(2\vartheta)(a_H^\dagger)^2\ket{0,0} &= \sqrt{2}\cos(2\vartheta)\sin(2\vartheta)\ket{2,0} \label{Transformation:terms_simple1}\\
    -\cos(2\vartheta)\sin(2\vartheta)(a_V^\dagger)^2\ket{0,0} &= -\sqrt{2}\cos(2\vartheta)\sin(2\vartheta)\ket{0,2} \label{Transformation:terms_simple2}\\
    -\cos^2(2\vartheta)a_H^\dagger a_V^\dagger\ket{0,0} &= -\cos^2(2\vartheta)\ket{1,1} \label{Transformation:terms_simple3}\\
    \sin^2(2\vartheta)a_H^\dagger a_V^\dagger\ket{0,0} &= \sin^2(2\vartheta)\ket{1,1}\label{Transformation:terms_simple4}
\end{align}

Remark on notation: Equations (\ref{Transformation:terms_simple1})-(\ref{Transformation:terms_simple4}), the indices for polarization were left out and further wont be used anymore due to convenience. To further make it easier to differentiate which polarization is meant, the convention of choosing $H$ polarization for the first and $V$ polarization for the second mode when using the $\ket{\cdot}$-notation will strictly be followed (when using operator notation indices will be used).

Looking at the terms (\ref{Transformation:terms_simple1})-(\ref{Transformation:terms_simple4}), we see that for the $\vartheta=\pi/8+k\pi/4$ and $k=0,1,2,3,...$, the terms containing one $H$ and one $V$ photon cancel each other due to destructive interference. However, this case only happens under the following conditions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item[-] The $H$ and $V$ photons occupy the same spatial mode.
    \item[-] The photons must be indistinguishable in all degrees of freedom besides polarization.
\end{itemize}
Consequently, for $\vartheta=\pi/8$, the sum of terms (\ref{Transformation:terms_simple1})-(\ref{Transformation:terms_simple2}) becomes
\begin{align}
    \ket{1_H,1_V}&=a_H^\dagger a_V^\dagger\ket{0,0}\notag\\
    &\overset{(\ref{Tansformation:trafo}),\vartheta=\pi/8}{\Rightarrow}\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{2,0}-\ket{0,2}).
\end{align}

\section{Positive Operator-Valued Measure (POVM)}
The measurement process can be described rigorously using the formalism of POVMs. In this framework, measurements are described by measurement operators $M_i$ performed on quantum systems in the state $\ket{\Psi}$.  The probability $P(i)$ of obtaining outcome $i$ is given by
\begin{align}
    P(i) = \bra{\Psi}M^{\dagger}_iM_i\ket{\Psi}.
\end{align}
Defining
\begin{align}
    \Pi_i\equiv M^{\dagger}_iM_i,
\end{align}
$\Pi_i$ is a positive operator such that 
\begin{align}
    \sum_i \Pi_i = I, \quad P(i) = \bra{\Psi}M^{\dagger}_iM_i\ket{\Psi},\label{formula:completeness_relation}
\end{align}
with $I$ as the identity matrix. Following the relation in equation (\ref{formula:completeness_relation}), the set of operators $\Pi_i$ are known as POVM elements associated with the measurement, whereas the complete set $\{\Pi_i\}$ is known as a POVM and is completely sufficient to determine  the probabilities of the different measurement outcomes\cite[Sec.~2.2.6]{nielsen_chuang}.


\subsection{POVM of bucket detectors}
In this work, the coincidence probabilities of click detectors - also called bucket detectors - are of interest. A bucket detector distinguishes only between two possible outcomes: no click (no photons detected) and click (at least one photon detected). Following, the two-way-outcome POVM is $\{\Pi_0, \Pi_{\text{click}}\}$.

The no-click event corresponds to the situation in which the field is in the vacuum state. This requirement can be formulated as
\begin{align}
    \bra{n}\Pi_0\ket{n} =
    \begin{cases}
        1, & n = 0, \\
        0, & n > 0.
    \end{cases}
\end{align}
The unique operator satisfying this condition is the projector onto the vacuum state,
\begin{align}
    \Pi_0 = \ket{0}\bra{0}.
\end{align}
Using equation (\ref{formula:completeness_relation}), the click operator is then given by
\begin{align}
    \Pi_{\text{click}} = I - \ket{0}\bra{0}.
\end{align}
For two detectors measuring the horizontal and vertical modes, the coincidence measurement operator is given by
\begin{align}
    \Pi_{\text{coinc}} = (I - \ket{0}\bra{0})_H \otimes (I - \ket{0}\bra{0})_V.
\end{align}
Expanding this operator yields
\begin{align}
    \Pi_{\text{coinc}} = I \otimes I 
    - \ket{0}\bra{0} \otimes I 
    - I \otimes \ket{0}\bra{0} 
    + \ket{0,0}\bra{0,0}.
\end{align}

Using the expansion of $\Pi_{\text{coinc}}$, the probability of measuring coincidences with bucket detectors can be expressed as
\begin{align}
P_{\text{coinc}} &= \bra{\Psi} I \otimes I \ket{\Psi}
- \bra{\Psi} (|0\rangle\langle 0| \otimes I) \ket{\Psi} \notag \\
&\quad - \bra{\Psi} (I \otimes |0\rangle\langle 0|) \ket{\Psi}
+ \bra{\Psi} |0,0\rangle\langle 0,0| \ket{\Psi}.
\end{align}
The individual terms evaluate to
\begin{align}
\bra{\Psi} I \otimes I \ket{\Psi} &= 1, \\
\bra{\Psi} (|0\rangle\langle 0| \otimes I) \ket{\Psi} &= P_H(0), \\
\bra{\Psi} (I \otimes |0\rangle\langle 0|) \ket{\Psi} &= P_V(0), \\
\bra{\Psi} |0,0\rangle\langle 0,0| \ket{\Psi} &= P(0,0),
\end{align}

which leads to
\begin{align}
P_{\text{coinc}} = 1 - P_H(0) - P_V(0) + P(0,0)\label{formula:POVM_cc_probability},
\end{align}

where $P_H(0)$ and $P_V(0)$ denote the probabilities of detecting no photons in the respective modes, and $P(0,0)$ is the joint vacuum probability. 

\subsection{Inclusion of loss}

In realistic experimental scenarios, losses due to imperfect coupling, optical elements, and detector inefficiencies must be taken into account. These losses can be modeled as linear attenuation processes and are equivalent to introducing fictitious beam splitters with transmission $\eta$ in front of each detector.

Within the POVM framework, loss can be incorporated directly into the measurement operators. For a detector with efficiency $\eta$, the no-click operator becomes
\begin{align}
\label{POVM:loss}
    \Pi_0^{(\eta)} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (1-\eta)^{\hat n} \ket{n}\bra{n},
\end{align}
which reflects the probability that all photons are lost before detection.

The corresponding click operator is then
\begin{align}
    \Pi_{\text{click}}^{(\eta)} = I - \Pi_0^{(\eta)}.
\end{align}

The coincidence operator in the presence of loss is given by
\begin{align}
    \Pi_{\text{coinc}}^{(\eta)} = (I - \Pi_0^{(\eta)})_H \otimes (I - \Pi_0^{(\eta)})_V.
\end{align}

Analogous to (\ref{formula:POVM_cc_probability}), the coincidence probability becomes
\begin{align}
\label{POVM:coinc_prob_loss}
    P_{\text{coinc}}^{(\eta)} = 1 - P_H^{(\eta)}(0) - P_V^{(\eta)}(0) + P^{(\eta)}(0,0),
\end{align}
where the vacuum probabilities now include the effect of loss.

For symmetric losses in both modes, a single effective efficiency $\eta$ can be used to describe all linear loss processes in the system.

For non-symmetric losses on detectors $A$ and $B$, this becomes:
\begin{align}
P_{\text{coinc}}^{(\eta_A,\eta_B)} &= \bra{\Psi}\Pi_{\text{click}}^{\eta_A}\otimes\Pi_{\text{click}}^{\eta_B}\ket{\Psi}\notag\\
&=\bra{\Psi}\left[\mathbb{1}-\left(1-\eta_A\right)^{\hat{n}_A}\right]\otimes \left[\mathbb{1}-\left(1-\eta_B\right)^{\hat{n}_B}\right]\ket{\Psi}\notag\\
&=\notag\\
&+1\notag\\
&-\bra{\Psi}\mathbb{1}\otimes\left(1-\eta_B\right)^{\hat{n}_B}\ket{\Psi}\notag\\
&-\bra{\Psi}\left(1-\eta_A\right)^{\hat{n}_A}\otimes \mathbb{1}\ket{\Psi}\notag\\
&+\bra{\Psi}\left(1-\eta_A\right)^{\hat{n}_A}\otimes \left(1-\eta_B\right)^{\hat{n}_B}\ket{\Psi}\\
&= 1 - P_H^{(\eta_A,\eta_B)}(0) - P_V^{(\eta_A,\eta_B)}(0) + P^{(\eta_A,\eta_B)}(0,0)\label{formula_P_cc_loss}
\end{align}

\section{\label{sec:s2} Discussion involving the Two-Mode Squeezed Vacuum}
The next step is to do the same steps which we did for the state in (\ref{initial_state_simple}) for the TMSV. In this section, we first will look at the mathematical formulation of the TMSV, how it behaves when transformed by the HWP, and how relevant detection probabilities can be derived using the POVM for lossy bucket detectors.
\subsection{Mathematical description of the two-mode squeezed vacuum}
For the correct phase-matching conditions the previously mentioned conditions of spatial overlap and indistinguishability are met. Mathematically, the TMSV state can be expressed as
\begin{align}
\label{tmsv}
    \ket{\text{TMSV}}&=\sqrt{1-|\lambda|^2}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\lambda^n\ket{n,n}\\
                     &=\sqrt{1-|\lambda|^2}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{\lambda^n}{n!}\left(a_H^\dagger\right)^n\left(a_V^\dagger\right)^n\ket{0,0},
\end{align}
with $n$ as the photon number and
\begin{align}
    \lambda=\tanh(r),
\end{align}
where $r$ is the squeezing parameter. 
\subsection{Rewriting the TMSV}
Sending the TMSV through a HWP results in
\begin{align}
\label{TMSV_HWP:full}
\ket{\text{TMSV}_{\text{HWP}}}=\Lambda&\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{\lambda^n}{n!}\left(\cos(2\vartheta)a_H^\dagger+\sin(2\vartheta)a_V^\dagger\right)^n\notag\\ &\times\left(\sin(2\vartheta)a_H^\dagger-\cos(2\vartheta)a_V^\dagger\right)^n\ket{0,0}.
\end{align}

Using the shorthand notation
\begin{equation}
c:=\cos(2\vartheta),
\qquad
s:=\sin(2\vartheta),
\end{equation}

(\ref{TMSV_HWP:full}) can be transformed into
\begin{equation}
|\Psi\rangle
=
\Lambda \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}
\frac{\lambda^n}{n!}
\left(c\,\hat a_H^\dagger + s\,\hat a_V^\dagger\right)^n
\left(s\,\hat a_H^\dagger - c\,\hat a_V^\dagger\right)^n
|0,0\rangle.
\label{eq:TMSV_HWP_sum}
\end{equation}

We now define the operator
\begin{equation}
\hat K^\dagger
:=
\left(c\,\hat a_H^\dagger + s\,\hat a_V^\dagger\right)
\left(s\,\hat a_H^\dagger - c\,\hat a_V^\dagger\right).
\label{eq:Kdag_def}
\end{equation}

Since all creation operators commute,
\begin{equation}
[\hat a_H^\dagger,\hat a_V^\dagger]=0,
\end{equation}
the two factors in \eqref{eq:Kdag_def} commute as well. Hence,
\begin{align}
(\hat K^\dagger)^n
& =
\left[
\left(c\,\hat a_H^\dagger + s\,\hat a_V^\dagger\right)
\left(s\,\hat a_H^\dagger - c\,\hat a_V^\dagger\right)
\right]^n \notag \\
& =
\left(c\,\hat a_H^\dagger + s\,\hat a_V^\dagger\right)^n
\left(s\,\hat a_H^\dagger - c\,\hat a_V^\dagger\right)^n.
\end{align}

Substituting this into \eqref{eq:TMSV_HWP_sum} gives
\begin{equation}
|\Psi\rangle
=
\Lambda \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}
\frac{\lambda^n}{n!}
(\hat K^\dagger)^n
|0,0\rangle.
\end{equation}

Using the Taylor series of the exponential,
\begin{equation}
e^{\lambda \hat K^\dagger}
=
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}
\frac{(\lambda \hat K^\dagger)^n}{n!}
=
\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}
\frac{\lambda^n}{n!}
(\hat K^\dagger)^n,
\end{equation}
we finally obtain the compact exponential form
\begin{equation}\label{formula:tmsv_as_exp}
\boxed{
|\Psi\rangle
=
\Lambda\, e^{\lambda \hat K^\dagger}|0,0\rangle,
 }
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\hat K^\dagger
=
\left(c\,\hat a_H^\dagger + s\,\hat a_V^\dagger\right)
\left(s\,\hat a_H^\dagger - c\,\hat a_V^\dagger\right).
}
\end{equation}

If desired, one may also expand $\hat K^\dagger$ explicitly:
\begin{align}
\hat K^\dagger
&=
\left(c\,\hat a_H^\dagger + s\,\hat a_V^\dagger\right)
\left(s\,\hat a_H^\dagger - c\,\hat a_V^\dagger\right) \\
&=
cs\,(\hat a_H^\dagger)^2
-c^2 \hat a_H^\dagger \hat a_V^\dagger
+s^2 \hat a_V^\dagger \hat a_H^\dagger
-cs\,(\hat a_V^\dagger)^2.
\end{align}
Because
\begin{equation}
\hat a_V^\dagger \hat a_H^\dagger
=
\hat a_H^\dagger \hat a_V^\dagger,
\end{equation}
this becomes
\begin{equation}
\hat K^\dagger
=
cs\,(\hat a_H^\dagger)^2
+
(s^2-c^2)\hat a_H^\dagger \hat a_V^\dagger
-cs\,(\hat a_V^\dagger)^2.
\end{equation}
Using
\begin{equation}
2cs=\sin(4\vartheta),
\qquad
s^2-c^2=-\cos(4\vartheta),
\end{equation}
one may write
\begin{equation}
\hat K^\dagger
=
\frac{\sin(4\vartheta)}{2}(\hat a_H^\dagger)^2
-\cos(4\vartheta)\,\hat a_H^\dagger \hat a_V^\dagger
-\frac{\sin(4\vartheta)}{2}(\hat a_V^\dagger)^2.
\end{equation}

With
\begin{align}\label{eq:alpha_vec}
    \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}=
    \begin{pmatrix}
    a_{H}^\dagger  \\
    a_{V}^\dagger
    \end{pmatrix}\quad \text{and}\quad 
    M=
    \begin{pmatrix}
    2cs & s^2-c^2 \\
    s^2-c^2 & -2cs
    \end{pmatrix},
\end{align}
equation (\ref{formula:tmsv_as_exp}) becomes:
\begin{equation}\label{formula:tmsv_as_exp_2}
\boxed{
|\Psi\rangle
=
\Lambda\, e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}^TM\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}}|0,0\rangle,
}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Deriving the probabilities for coincidences}
To derive the analytical expression for the coincidence probability, expression (\ref{formula_P_cc_loss}) will be evaluated using the TMSV of the form depicted in (\ref{formula:tmsv_as_exp_2}). For now, the focus now shall only be the evaluation of the term
\begin{align}
P^{(\eta_H,\eta_V)}(0,0)=&\bra{\Psi}\left(1-\eta_H\right)^{\hat{n}_H}\otimes \left(1-\eta_V\right)^{\hat{n}_V}\ket{\Psi}\label{eq:p00etahv}\\
=&\Lambda^2\bra{0} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}} t_H^{\hat{n}_H} t_V^{\hat{n}_V} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} (\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T M \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}} \ket{0}\label{formula:last_term1}
\end{align}
where the abbreviations
\begin{align}\label{eq:t_eta}
    t_{H,V}=1-\eta_{H,V}
\end{align}
are used and the $\otimes$ was omitted for readability. Further, 
\begin{align}
S=t_H^{\hat{n}_H} t_V^{\hat{n}_V}
\end{align}
and
\begin{align}
D=
\begin{pmatrix}
    t_H & 0 \\
    0 & t_V 
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}
are introduced. It follows that
\begin{align}\label{formula:SaS_Da}
S\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}S^{-1}=D\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}},
\end{align}
and since $D$ is diagonal:
\begin{align}
\left(D\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}\right)^T=(\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T D
\end{align}
Due to the relations in (\ref{eq:x pow n a dagger}) and (\ref{eq:x pow n a dagger2}) we get:
\begin{align}
S\hat a_{H,V}^{\dagger}S^{-1}=t_{H,V}\hat a_{H,V}^{\dagger}.
\end{align}
Then (\ref{formula:last_term1}) becomes
\begin{align}
&\Lambda^2\bra{0} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}} t_H^{\hat{n}_H} t_V^{\hat{n}_V} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} (\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T M \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}} \ket{0}\\
=&\Lambda^2\bra{0} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}} S e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} (\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T M \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}} \ket{0}\\
=&\Lambda^2\bra{0} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}} S e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} (\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T M \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}}S^{-1}S \ket{0}\\
=&\Lambda^2\bra{0} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} S (\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T M \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}S^{-1}}S \ket{0},
\end{align}
which is achieved by inserting $S^{-1}S=\mathbf{1}$ the relation (\ref{appendix:powerseries_final}). By inserting $S^{-1}S=\mathbf{1}$ and using (\ref{formula:SaS_Da}) into the exponent we get
\begin{align}
&\Lambda^2\bra{0} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} S (\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T M \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}S^{-1}}S \ket{0}\\
=&\Lambda^2\bra{0} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} (S (\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T S^{-1}) M (S \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}S^{-1})}S \ket{0}\\
=&\Lambda^2\bra{0} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} (D\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T M D\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}}S \ket{0}\\
=&\Lambda^2\bra{0} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} (\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T D M D\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}}S \ket{0}.\label{formula:tmsv_DaMDa}
\end{align}
Here, we made use of the fact that you can expand the indices of $(\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T M \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}$ which results in:
\begin{align}
&S\left((\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T M \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}\right)S^{-1}\\=&S\left(\sum_{i,j}M_{i,j}\hat a_i^{\dagger}\hat a_j^{\dagger}\right)S^{-1}\\
=&\sum_{i,j}M_{i,j}S\hat a_i^{\dagger}\hat a_j^{\dagger}S^{-1}\\
=&\sum_{i,j}M_{i,j}\left(S\hat a_i^{\dagger}S^{-1}\right)\left(S\hat a_j^{\dagger}S^{-1}\right)\\
=&\left(S(\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^TS^{-1}\right)M\left(S\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}S^{-1}\right)
\end{align}
By further using (\ref{eq:x pow n m}) we can see that
\begin{align}
S\ket{0}=\ket{0},
\end{align}
which gives us
\begin{align}
&\Lambda^2\bra{0} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}} t_H^{\hat{n}_H} t_V^{\hat{n}_V} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} (\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T M \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}} \ket{0}\\
=&\Lambda^2\bra{0} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}}  e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} (\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T DMD \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}} \ket{0}\label{eq:tmsv_aDMDa}
\end{align}

Using die identity from (\ref{eq:coherent_identity}), expression (\ref{eq:tmsv_aDMDa}) becomes
\begin{align}
&\Lambda^2\bra{0} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}}
e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} (\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T L \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}}\ket{0} \\
=&\Lambda^2\int \dfrac{d^4\alpha}{\pi^2}   \bra{0} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}}\ket{\alpha}\bra{\alpha}e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} (\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T L \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}} \ket{0}\label{eq:tmsv_coherent_identity},
\end{align}
with $DMD=L$ as an abbreviation. Also note that $\pi$ and $d^2\alpha$ are squared since we have a to modes:
\begin{align}
\ket{\alpha}=\ket{\alpha_H,\alpha_V}
\end{align}

By inserting the coherent-state resolution of the identity, the operator expression is transformed into an integral over phase-space variables. This reformulation introduces classical field amplitudes as integration variables, while the underlying quantum correlations remain encoded in the resulting Gaussian weight. 

In order to evaluate the expression (\ref{eq:tmsv_coherent_identity}), we first need to show how $\mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}$ acts on $\ket{\alpha}$. We can write 
\begin{align}
\hat a_{H}\ket{\alpha_H,\alpha_V}&=\alpha_{H}\ket{\alpha_H,\alpha_V},\\
\hat a_{V}\ket{\alpha_H,\alpha_V}&=\alpha_{V}\ket{\alpha_H,\alpha_V}.
\end{align}
Then we can write $\mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}$ as
\begin{align}\label{eq:ama_1}
&\mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}\\
&=\sum_{i,j}\hat a_i M_{i,j}\hat a_j\\
&=2cs\hat a_H^2+(s^2-c^2)\hat a_H\hat a_V+(s^2-c^2)\hat a_V\hat a_H-2cs\hat a_V^2.
\end{align}
Since the annihilation operators commute
\begin{align}
\left[\hat a_H,\hat a_V\right]=0,
\end{align}
we can shorten expression (\ref{eq:ama_1}) a little bit:
\begin{align}
&\dfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}\\
=&cs\hat a_H^2+(s^2-c^2)\hat a_H\hat a_V-cs\hat a_V^2
\end{align}

Consequently, we can write
\begin{align}
    &\mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}\ket{\alpha}\\
    =&\left(cs\hat a_H^2+(s^2-c^2)\hat a_H\hat a_V-cs\hat a_V^2\right)\ket{\alpha_H,\alpha_V}\\
    =&\left(cs\alpha_H^2+(s^2-c^2)\alpha_H\alpha_V-cs\alpha_V^2\right)\ket{\alpha_H,\alpha_V}\\
    =&\alpha^TM\alpha\ket{\alpha}
\end{align}
Following that we can see how the exponential of (\ref{eq:tmsv_coherent_identity}) evaluates by writing it as an infinite sum:
\begin{align}
    \bra{0}e^{\frac{\lambda}{2}\mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}}\ket{\alpha}&=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\dfrac{(\frac{\lambda}{2}\mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a})^n}{n!}\braket{0|\alpha}\\
    &=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\dfrac{(\frac{\lambda}{2}\alpha^TM\alpha)^n}{n!}\braket{0|\alpha}\\
    &=e^{\frac{\lambda}{2}\alpha^TM\alpha}\braket{0|\alpha}
\end{align}
For a normalized two-mode coherent state we can evaluate $\braket{0|\alpha}$ (with the help of the expressions (\ref{formula:coherent_state matrix}) and (\ref{eq:alpha_vec})) to
\begin{align}
\braket{0|\alpha}=e^{-\frac{|\alpha_H|^2}{2}}e^{-\frac{|\alpha_V|^2}{2}}=e^{-\frac{\alpha^{\dagger}\alpha}{2}}.
\end{align}
Following, we can see that 
\begin{align}
\bra{0}e^{\frac{\lambda}{2}\mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}}\ket{\alpha}
=e^{\frac{\lambda}{2}\alpha^TM\alpha-\frac{\alpha^{\dagger}\alpha}{2}},
\end{align}
and following a similar logic, we can see that
\begin{align}
\bra{\alpha}e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} (\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T L \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}} \ket{0}=e^{\frac{\lambda}{2}(\alpha^*)^TL\alpha^*-\frac{\alpha^{\dagger}\alpha}{2}}.
\end{align}
As an intermediate step, we now can rewrite (\ref{eq:tmsv_coherent_identity}) as
\begin{align}
&\Lambda^2\bra{0} e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} \mathbf{\hat a}^T M\mathbf{\hat a}}
e^{\frac{\lambda}{2} (\mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}})^T L \mathbf{\hat a^{\dagger}}}\ket{0} \notag\\
=&\Lambda^2\int \dfrac{d^4\alpha}{\pi^2}   e^{\frac{\lambda}{2}(\alpha^*)^TL\alpha^* +\frac{\lambda}{2}\alpha^TM\alpha-\alpha^{\dagger}\alpha}\label{eq:intermediate}
\end{align}

Introduce the real variables
\begin{align}
\alpha=\frac{x+iy}{\sqrt{2}},
\qquad
\alpha^*=\frac{x-iy}{\sqrt{2}},
\qquad
x,y\in\mathbb R^2,
\end{align}
and define
\begin{align}
\xi:=
\begin{pmatrix}
x\\
y
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
x_H\\
x_V\\
y_H\\
y_V\\
\end{pmatrix}
\in\mathbb R^4.
\end{align}

Then
\begin{align}
\chi:=
\begin{pmatrix}
\alpha\\
\alpha^*
\end{pmatrix}
=
W\xi,
\qquad
W:=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathbb{1} & i\mathbb{1}\\
\mathbb{1} & -i\mathbb{1}
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{align}

Moreover, the exponent can be written as
\begin{align}
\frac{\lambda}{2}(\alpha^*)^T L\,\alpha^*
+
\frac{\lambda}{2}\alpha^T M\,\alpha
-
\alpha^\dagger \alpha
=
-\frac12\,\chi^T Q\,\chi,
\end{align}
with
\begin{align}
Q=
\begin{pmatrix}
-\lambda M & \mathbb{1}\\
\mathbb{1} & -\lambda L
\end{pmatrix}\label{eq:Q}.
\end{align}

Using \(\chi=W\xi\), this becomes
\begin{align}
-\frac12\,\chi^T Q\,\chi
=
-\frac12\,\xi^T W^T Q W\,\xi.
\end{align}

Hence, defining
\begin{align}
A:=W^T Q W,
\end{align}
we obtain
\begin{align}
I
=
\int \frac{d^2\alpha}{\pi^2}\,
\exp\!\left(-\frac12\,\xi^T A\,\xi\right),
\end{align}
with $I$ being only the integral of (\ref{eq:intermediate}).\\
Now the measure transforms as
\begin{align}
d^4\alpha
=
d^2\alpha_H\,d^2\alpha_V
=
\frac14\,dx_H\,dy_H\,dx_V\,dy_V
=
\frac14\,d^4\xi,
\end{align}
so that
\begin{align}
\frac{d^4\alpha}{\pi^2}
=
\frac{d^4\xi}{(2\pi)^2}.
\end{align}

Therefore,
\begin{align}
I
=
\int \frac{d^4\xi}{(2\pi)^2}
\exp\!\left(-\frac12\,\xi^T A\,\xi\right),
\qquad
A=W^T Q W.
\end{align}

Evaluating \(A\) explicitly gives
\begin{align}
A&=
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathbb{1}-\dfrac{\lambda}{2}(L+M) & \dfrac{i\lambda}{2}(L-M)\\[2mm]
\dfrac{i\lambda}{2}(L-M) & \mathbb{1}+\dfrac{\lambda}{2}(L+M)
\end{pmatrix}\\
&=
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathbb{1}-\dfrac{\lambda}{2}(L+M) & 0\\[2mm]
0 & \mathbb{1}+\dfrac{\lambda}{2}(L+M)
\end{pmatrix}\\
&+
i\begin{pmatrix}
0 & \dfrac{\lambda}{2}(L-M)\\[2mm]
\dfrac{\lambda}{2}(L-M) & 0
\end{pmatrix}\\
&=B+iC=\mathbb{R}(A)+i\mathbb{I}(A)
\end{align}

\subsection{Evaluation of the complex Gaussian integral}

We now evaluate
\begin{align}
I
=
\int \frac{d^4\xi}{(2\pi)^2}
\exp\!\left(-\frac12\,\xi^T A\,\xi\right),
\qquad
A=W^TQW.
\end{align}
Since $A$ is not purely real, we write it as
\begin{align}
A=B+iC,
\end{align}
where $B=\mathbb{R}(A)$ and $C=\mathbb{I}(A)$ are real $4\times4$ matrices. From the explicit form of $A$ we see that both $B$ and $C$ are symmetric, hence
\begin{align}
A^T=A,\qquad B^T=B,\qquad C^T=C.
\end{align}
Therefore,
\begin{align}
\xi^T A\xi=\xi^T B\xi+i\,\xi^T C\xi.
\end{align}
The integral thus becomes
\begin{align}
I
=
\int \frac{d^4\xi}{(2\pi)^2}
\exp\!\left(-\frac12\,\xi^T B\xi\right)
\exp\!\left(-\frac{i}{2}\,\xi^T C\xi\right).
\end{align}
As long as $B$ is positive definite, the integral converges absolutely, since
\begin{align}
\left|
\exp\!\left(-\frac12\,\xi^T A\xi\right)
\right|
=
\exp\!\left(-\frac12\,\xi^T B\xi\right).
\end{align}

Because $B$ is real symmetric and positive definite, it possesses a real symmetric square root $B^{1/2}$ with inverse $B^{-1/2}$. We may therefore factorize $A$ as
\begin{align}
A
=
B^{1/2}\left(\mathbb{1}+iK\right)B^{1/2},
\qquad
K:=B^{-1/2}CB^{-1/2}.
\end{align}
Since $C$ is real symmetric, $K$ is also real symmetric:
\begin{align}
K^T=K.
\end{align}

Next, we perform the change of variables
\begin{align}
y=B^{1/2}\xi,
\qquad
\xi=B^{-1/2}y.
\end{align}
The Jacobian of this transformation is
\begin{align}
d^4\xi=\frac{d^4y}{\sqrt{\det B}}.
\end{align}
Moreover,
\begin{align}
\xi^T A\xi
&=\xi^T B^{1/2}\left(\mathbb{1}+iK\right)B^{1/2}\xi\\
&=y^T\left(\mathbb{1}+iK\right)y.
\end{align}
Hence the integral becomes
\begin{align}
I
=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{\det B}}
\int \frac{d^4y}{(2\pi)^2}
\exp\!\left[
-\frac12\,y^T\left(\mathbb{1}+iK\right)y
\right].
\end{align}

Since $K$ is real symmetric, it can be diagonalized by an orthogonal matrix. Thus, there exists\cite{axler2024linear} an orthogonal matrix $O$ such that
\begin{align}
K=O^T\Lambda O,
\qquad
\Lambda=\mathrm{diag}(\kappa_1,\kappa_2,\kappa_3,\kappa_4),
\end{align}
with real eigenvalues $\kappa_j$. We now define
\begin{align}
z=Oy.
\end{align}
Because $O$ is orthogonal, we have
\begin{align}
d^4y=d^4z,
\qquad
y^Ty=z^Tz.
\end{align}
Furthermore,
\begin{align}
y^T\left(\mathbb{1}+iK\right)y
&=
y^Ty+i\,y^TKy\\
&=
z^Tz+i\,z^T\Lambda z\\
&=
\sum_{j=1}^4 (1+i\kappa_j)z_j^2.
\end{align}
Therefore, the integral factorizes into four one-dimensional Gaussian integrals:
\begin{align}
I
=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{\det B}}
\prod_{j=1}^4
\int \frac{dz_j}{\sqrt{2\pi}}
\exp\!\left[
-\frac12(1+i\kappa_j)z_j^2
\right].
\end{align}

Each factor is a convergent Gaussian integral because
\begin{align}
\mathbb{R}(1+i\kappa_j)=1>0.
\end{align}
Using the standard Gaussian formula
\begin{align}
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{dz}{\sqrt{2\pi}}
\exp\!\left(-\frac{a}{2}z^2\right)
=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{a}},
\qquad
\mathbb{R}(a)>0,
\end{align}
we obtain
\begin{align}
\int \frac{dz_j}{\sqrt{2\pi}}
\exp\!\left[
-\frac12(1+i\kappa_j)z_j^2
\right]
=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+i\kappa_j}}.
\end{align}
Thus,
\begin{align}
I
&=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{\det B}}
\prod_{j=1}^4 \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+i\kappa_j}}\\
&=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{\det B}}
\frac{1}{\sqrt{\prod_{j=1}^4 (1+i\kappa_j)}}.
\end{align}
Since
\begin{align}
\prod_{j=1}^4 (1+i\kappa_j)
=
\det(\mathbb{1}+iK),
\end{align}
this becomes
\begin{align}
I
=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{\det B\,\det(\mathbb{1}+iK)}}.
\end{align}

Finally, using
\begin{align}
A
=
B^{1/2}\left(\mathbb{1}+iK\right)B^{1/2},
\end{align}
we get
\begin{align}
\det A
&=
\det(B^{1/2})\det(\mathbb{1}+iK)\det(B^{1/2})\\
&=
\det(B)\det(\mathbb{1}+iK).
\end{align}
Therefore,
\begin{align}
\boxed{
I
=
\int \frac{d^4\xi}{(2\pi)^2}
\exp\!\left(-\frac12\,\xi^T A\,\xi\right)
=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{\det A}}.
}
\end{align}

After transforming the expression into a real quadratic form, the problem reduces to a multivariate Gaussian integral. Expressions of this form are equivalent to those encountered in the phase-space description of Gaussian states via the Wigner function formalism, where the matrix $A$ can be interpreted as an effective inverse covariance matrix~\cite{ferraro2005gaussianstatescontinuousvariable,brask2022gaussianstatesoperations}.

While this connection provides useful conceptual insight, the following calculation is carried out within the present operator-based framework in order to maintain a direct and explicit derivation. In order to evaluate $\det A$, we recall that $A=W^TQW$. Consequently, the determinant of $A$ is
\begin{align}
\det A &= \det(W^TQW)\\
&=\det (W)^2\det Q\\
&=\det Q,
\end{align}
with $\det(W)^2=\mathbb 1$. Using (\ref{appendix:det_of_blockmatrix}) and (\ref{eq:Q}), we are able to rewrite $\det A$ as:
\begin{align}
\det A&=\det(\lambda^2ML-\mathbb{1})\\
&=\det(\mathbb{1}-\lambda^2ML)\\
&=\det(\mathbb{1}-\lambda^2MDMD)
\end{align}

\subsection*{Interpretation of the no-click and coincidence probabilities}

For two bucket detectors measuring the $H$ and $V$ output modes, each detector has only two possible outcomes:
\emph{click} or \emph{no click}. Therefore, in each trial there are four mutually exclusive joint outcomes:
\begin{align}
&\text{(i) no click in either arm}, \nonumber\\
&\text{(ii) only the $H$ detector clicks}, \nonumber\\
&\text{(iii) only the $V$ detector clicks}, \nonumber\\
&\text{(iv) both detectors click (coincidence).} \nonumber
\end{align}

It is useful to assign probabilities to these four elementary outcomes:
\begin{align}
P_{00} &:= \Pr(\text{no click in $H$ and no click in $V$}),\\
P_{H\text{-only}} &:= \Pr(\text{$H$ clicks and $V$ does not click}),\\
P_{V\text{-only}} &:= \Pr(\text{$V$ clicks and $H$ does not click}),\\
P_{\mathrm{cc}} &:= \Pr(\text{$H$ clicks and $V$ clicks}).
\end{align}
Since these four events are disjoint and exhaustive, their probabilities must sum to unity:
\begin{equation}
1 = P_{00} + P_{H\text{-only}} + P_{V\text{-only}} + P_{\mathrm{cc}}.
\label{eq:four_outcomes_sum}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Connection to the notation $P_H^{(\eta)}(0)$, $P_V^{(\eta)}(0)$, and $P^{(\eta_H,\eta_V)}(0,0)$.}

The quantity
\begin{equation}
P_H^{(\eta_H)}(0)
=
\langle \Psi | (1-\eta_H)^{\hat n_H}\otimes I |\Psi\rangle
\end{equation}
is the probability that the $H$ detector does \emph{not} click, irrespective of what happens in the $V$ arm. Hence it contains two of the four elementary outcomes:
\begin{equation}
P_H^{(\eta_H)}(0) = P_{00} + P_{V\text{-only}}.
\label{eq:PH_meaning}
\end{equation}

Similarly,
\begin{equation}
P_V^{(\eta_V)}(0)
=
\langle \Psi | I\otimes (1-\eta_V)^{\hat n_V} |\Psi\rangle
\end{equation}
is the probability that the $V$ detector does \emph{not} click, regardless of what happens in the $H$ arm, so that
\begin{equation}
P_V^{(\eta_V)}(0) = P_{00} + P_{H\text{-only}}.
\label{eq:PV_meaning}
\end{equation}

Finally, the joint no-click probability is
\begin{equation}
P^{(\eta_H,\eta_V)}(0,0)
=
\langle \Psi | (1-\eta_H)^{\hat n_H}\otimes (1-\eta_V)^{\hat n_V} |\Psi\rangle,
\end{equation}
which is precisely the probability that neither detector clicks:
\begin{equation}
P^{(\eta_H,\eta_V)}(0,0)=P_{00}.
\label{eq:P00_meaning}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Connection to the coincidence probability.}

The coincidence probability is the probability that both detectors click:
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{coinc}}
=
P_{\mathrm{cc}}.
\end{equation}
Using Eqs.~\eqref{eq:PH_meaning}--\eqref{eq:P00_meaning}, it follows that
\begin{equation}
P_{\mathrm{coinc}}
=
1 - P_H^{(\eta_H)}(0) - P_V^{(\eta_V)}(0) + P^{(\eta_H,\eta_V)}(0,0),
\label{eq:Pcoinc_inclusion_exclusion}
\end{equation}
which is just the inclusion--exclusion formula for the event that both detectors click.

\paragraph{Connection to the lossless notation.}

In the lossless discussion, one often groups the four elementary outcomes into only three classes:
\begin{align}
P_{\text{no-click}} &:= P_{00},\\
P_{\text{1-click}} &:= P_{H\text{-only}} + P_{V\text{-only}},\\
P_{\text{coinc.}} &:= P_{\mathrm{cc}}.
\end{align}
Equation~\eqref{eq:four_outcomes_sum} then becomes
\begin{equation}
1 \overset{!}{=} P_{\text{no-click}} + P_{\text{1-click}} + P_{\text{coinc.}}.
\label{eq:lossless_grouped}
\end{equation}

Thus, the quantities $P_H^{(\eta_H)}(0)$ and $P_V^{(\eta_V)}(0)$ are not additional detector outcomes by themselves. Rather, they are \emph{marginal no-click probabilities}, i.e.\ partial sums over the four elementary outcomes:
\begin{align}
P_H^{(\eta_H)}(0) &= P_{\text{no-click}} + P_{V\text{-only}},\\
P_V^{(\eta_V)}(0) &= P_{\text{no-click}} + P_{H\text{-only}}.
\end{align}

\section{POVM as a function of the number operator}
Considering the operator
\begin{equation}
A := \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} x^k \, |k\rangle\langle k|.
\end{equation}

The Fock states $|k\rangle$ are eigenstates of the number operator
\begin{equation}
\hat{n} = \hat{a}^\dagger \hat{a}, 
\qquad 
\hat{n} |k\rangle = k |k\rangle.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Why $x^{\hat{n}}|m\rangle = x^m |m\rangle$}

For any function $f$, the operator $f(\hat{n})$ is defined via its power series. 
In particular,
\begin{equation}
x^{\hat{n}} = e^{(\ln x)\hat{n}}
= \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \frac{(\ln x)^j}{j!} \, \hat{n}^{\,j}.
\end{equation}

Since $|m\rangle$ is an eigenstate of $\hat{n}$, we have
\begin{equation}
\hat{n}^{\,j} |m\rangle = m^j |m\rangle,
\end{equation}
and therefore
\begin{align}
x^{\hat{n}} |m\rangle
&= \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \frac{(\ln x)^j}{j!} \, m^j |m\rangle \\
&= \left( \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \frac{(\ln x)^j m^j}{j!} \right) |m\rangle \\
&= e^{m \ln x} |m\rangle \\
&= x^m |m\rangle.
\end{align}

\paragraph{Action of $A$ on number states}

Acting with $A$ on an arbitrary number state $|m\rangle$ yields
\begin{align}
A|m\rangle
&= \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} x^k |k\rangle \langle k|m\rangle \\
&= \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} x^k |k\rangle \delta_{k,m} \\
&= x^m |m\rangle.
\end{align}

Since both operators act identically on all basis states, we conclude
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} x^k |k\rangle\langle k| = x^{\hat{n}}.
}
\end{equation}

In particular, for $x = 1-\eta$,
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\Pi_0^{(\eta)} = (1-\eta)^{\hat{n}}.
}
\end{equation}

\section{Determinant identity for \texorpdfstring{$\det(\mathbb{1}-\lambda^2 X)$}{det(I-lambdaÂ˛X)}}\label{appendix:det_identity}

In this appendix, we derive a useful identity for the determinant of a $2\times 2$ matrix of the form
\begin{align}
\det(\mathbb{1}-\lambda^2 X),
\end{align}
where $X$ is an arbitrary $2\times2$ matrix.

Let
\begin{align}
X=
\begin{pmatrix}
a & b\\
c & d
\end{pmatrix}
\quad
(X\in\mathbb{C}^{2\times2}).
\end{align}
Then
\begin{align}
\mathbb{1}-\lambda^2X
=
\begin{pmatrix}
1-\lambda^2 a & -\lambda^2 b\\
-\lambda^2 c & 1-\lambda^2 d
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{align}
Using the determinant formula for a $2\times2$ matrix,
\begin{align}
\det
\begin{pmatrix}
p&q\\
r&s
\end{pmatrix}
=ps-qr,
\end{align}
we obtain
\begin{align}
\det(\mathbb{1}-\lambda^2X)
&=
(1-\lambda^2 a)(1-\lambda^2 d)-(-\lambda^2 b)(-\lambda^2 c)\\
&=
(1-\lambda^2 a)(1-\lambda^2 d)-\lambda^4bc\\
&=
1-\lambda^2(a+d)+\lambda^4(ad-bc).
\end{align}
Now recall that for the matrix $X$,
\begin{align}
\Tr(X)=a+d,
\qquad
\det(X)=ad-bc.
\end{align}
Therefore:
\begin{align}\label{appendix:det_1minusl2X}
\boxed{
\det(\mathbb{1}-\lambda^2X)
=
1-\lambda^2\Tr(X)+\lambda^4\det(X)
}
\end{align}
Thus, for any $2\times2$ matrix $X$, the determinant of $\mathbb{1}-\lambda^2X$ can always be written in terms of the trace and determinant of $X$.

\section{Positive definiteness of the matrix $B$}

In this appendix we provide a detailed justification that the matrix $B = \Re(A)$ introduced in Eq.~(112) is real symmetric and positive definite under physically relevant conditions. This property is essential for the convergence of the Gaussian integral in Sec.~IV D and for the existence of the factorization in Eq.~(119).

\subsection{Structure of $B$}

From Eq.~(109)â€“(112), the matrix $A$ can be decomposed into its real and imaginary parts as
\begin{equation}
A = B + iC.
\end{equation}
The real part is explicitly given by
\begin{equation}
B =
\begin{pmatrix}
I - \frac{\lambda}{2}(L + M) & 0 \\
0 & I + \frac{\lambda}{2}(L + M)
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Here, $M$ is defined in Eq.~(50), $D = \mathrm{diag}(t_H, t_V)$ in Eq.~(56), and $L = DMD$.

We first observe that both $M$ and $D$ are real symmetric matrices. Since the product of symmetric matrices of the form $DMD$ preserves symmetry, it follows that $L$ is also real symmetric. Consequently, $B$ is real symmetric as it consists of symmetric blocks on the diagonal.

\subsection{Spectral properties of $M$}

The matrix $M$ has the explicit form
\begin{equation}
M =
\begin{pmatrix}
2cs & s^2 - c^2 \\
s^2 - c^2 & -2cs
\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
where $c = \cos(2\vartheta)$ and $s = \sin(2\vartheta)$. A direct computation shows that
\begin{equation}
(2cs)^2 + (s^2 - c^2)^2 = 1,
\end{equation}
which implies
\begin{equation}
M^2 = \mathbb{1}.
\end{equation}

This relation has an important consequence for the spectrum of $M$. If $v$ is an eigenvector of $M$ with eigenvalue $\lambda$, then
\begin{equation}
M^2 v = \lambda^2 v.
\end{equation}
Since $M^2 = \mathbb{1}$, it follows that $\lambda^2 = 1$, and therefore $\lambda = \pm 1$. Thus, all eigenvalues of $M$ lie in the set $\{-1,1\}$.

Because $M$ is real symmetric, its operator norm is equal to the largest absolute value of its eigenvalues (see, e.g., \cite{GilbertIntroductionToLinearAlgebra2016}). Hence,
\begin{equation}
\|M\|_2 = 1.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Bound on $L = DMD$}

Next, we analyze the matrix $L = DMD$. The matrix $D = \mathrm{diag}(t_H, t_V)$ encodes detector losses, where $t_{H,V} = 1 - \eta_{H,V}$ and $0 \le \eta_{H,V} \le 1$. Therefore,
\begin{equation}
0 \le t_H, t_V \le 1.
\end{equation}
For diagonal matrices, the operator norm is given by the largest absolute diagonal entry, and hence
\begin{equation}
\|D\|_2 = \max(t_H, t_V) \le 1.
\end{equation}

Using the submultiplicativity of the operator norm,
\begin{equation}
\|AB\|_2 \le \|A\|_2 \|B\|_2,
\end{equation}
we obtain
\begin{equation}
\|L\|_2 = \|DMD\|_2 \le \|D\|_2^2 \|M\|_2 \le 1.
\end{equation}

We now consider the sum $L + M$. Using the triangle inequality,
\begin{equation}
\|L + M\|_2 \le \|L\|_2 + \|M\|_2 \le 2.
\end{equation}
Since $L+M$ is real symmetric, this bound implies that all of its eigenvalues lie in the interval $[-2,2]$.

\subsection{Positivity of $B$}

We now determine the eigenvalues of $B$. Due to its block-diagonal structure, the eigenvalues of $B$ are given by those of the two blocks
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{1} \pm \frac{\lambda}{2}(L+M).
\end{equation}

Let $v$ be an eigenvector of $L+M$ with eigenvalue $\mu$. Then
\begin{equation}
(L+M)v = \mu v.
\end{equation}
Applying the block matrices to $v$ yields
\begin{equation}
\left(\mathbb{1} \pm \frac{\lambda}{2}(L+M)\right)v
= \left(1 \pm \frac{\lambda}{2}\mu\right)v.
\end{equation}
Thus, the eigenvalues of $B$ are
\begin{equation}
\lambda_\pm = 1 \pm \frac{\lambda}{2}\mu.
\end{equation}

Since $\mu \in [-2,2]$, the smallest possible value of $\lambda_\pm$ occurs for $\mu = \pm 2$, yielding
\begin{equation}
\lambda_\pm \ge 1 - \lambda.
\end{equation}
For finite squeezing parameter $r$, we have $\lambda = \tanh(r)$ with $0 \le \lambda < 1$, and therefore
\begin{equation}
\lambda_\pm > 0.
\end{equation}

Hence, all eigenvalues of $B$ are strictly positive, and we conclude that $B$ is positive definite.

\subsection{Existence and uniqueness of $B^{1/2}$}
Since $B$ is real symmetric, the spectral theorem guarantees an orthogonal diagonalization
\begin{equation}
B = Q \Lambda Q^T,
\end{equation}
where $Q$ is orthogonal and $\Lambda = \mathrm{diag}(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n)$ contains the eigenvalues of $B$ \cite{GilbertIntroductionToLinearAlgebra2016}. Since $B$ is positive definite, all eigenvalues satisfy $\lambda_i > 0$.

We define
\begin{equation}
B^{1/2} := Q \Lambda^{1/2} Q^T,
\end{equation}
where $\Lambda^{1/2} = \mathrm{diag}(\sqrt{\lambda_1},\dots,\sqrt{\lambda_n})$. By construction,
\begin{equation}
B^{1/2} B^{1/2} = B.
\end{equation}

Since all eigenvalues $\lambda_i$ are strictly positive, their square roots are well-defined and nonzero, implying that $B^{1/2}$ is invertible. The inverse is given by
\begin{equation}
B^{-1/2} = Q \Lambda^{-1/2} Q^T.
\end{equation}

The square root constructed in this way is the unique real symmetric positive definite square root of $B$ (see \cite{GilbertIntroductionToLinearAlgebra2016}).

\subsection{Conclusion}

We have shown that $B$ is real symmetric and positive definite for all physically relevant parameters ($0 \le \eta_{H,V} \le 1$, finite squeezing $r$). This ensures both the convergence of the Gaussian integral and the validity of the factorization used in Eq.~(119).

\section{Operator norms}

In this appendix we briefly review the operator norm and provide the derivation of $\|M\|_2$ used in the main text.

\subsection{Definition of the operator norm}

For a matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, the operator norm induced by the Euclidean norm is defined as
\begin{equation}
\|A\|_2 := \sup_{x \neq 0} \frac{\|Ax\|_2}{\|x\|_2}.
\end{equation}
This norm measures the maximal stretching of vectors under the linear transformation defined by $A$.

An equivalent characterization is
\begin{equation}
\|A\|_2 = \sqrt{\lambda_{\max}(A^T A)},
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_{\max}$ denotes the largest eigenvalue. If $A$ is real symmetric, this simplifies to
\begin{equation}
\|A\|_2 = \max_i |\lambda_i(A)|,
\end{equation}
see \cite{GilbertIntroductionToLinearAlgebra2016}.

\subsection{Evaluation of $\|M\|_2$}

From the relation $M^2 = \mathbb{1}$, it follows that the eigenvalues of $M$ satisfy $\lambda^2 = 1$, and hence $\lambda = \pm 1$. Since $M$ is real symmetric, its operator norm is given by the largest absolute eigenvalue, and therefore
\begin{equation}
\|M\|_2 = 1.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Norm inequalities}

We make use of the standard inequalities
\begin{align}
\|AB\|_2 &\le \|A\|_2 \|B\|_2, \\
\|A + B\|_2 &\le \|A\|_2 + \|B\|_2,
\end{align}
see \cite{GilbertIntroductionToLinearAlgebra2016}.

For diagonal matrices $D = \mathrm{diag}(d_i)$, one has
\begin{equation}
\|D\|_2 = \max_i |d_i|.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Application to $L = DMD$}

Applying these results yields
\begin{equation}
\|L\|_2 = \|DMD\|_2 \le \|D\|_2^2 \|M\|_2 \le 1,
\end{equation}
which is used to bound the spectrum of $L+M$ and establish the positive definiteness of $B$.
